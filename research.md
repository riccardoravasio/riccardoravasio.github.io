<h1 class="page-title">Research</h1>

<section markdown="1">

  In competition experiments between variants of a DNA polymerase (Husain et al), we noticed that the more error-prone variants would lose even when inaccuracy was rewarded. This is because more error prone polymerases are also slower copiers. Fifty years of theory on error correction say the opposite: accuracy takes time. How do we get the opposite? One key ingredient was missing. Errors stall replication; correcting them is faster than paying the associated time cost. So kinetic proofreading and similar non-equilibrium error-correcting machineries can actually be seen as providing an overall boost, not a slowdown. With coarse-grained mechanistic models and the largest mutational screen of a DNA polymerase to date, we showed that selection for speed alone is enough to evolve elaborate error correction — even with no direct pressure on accuracy and no information to protect.
</section>
